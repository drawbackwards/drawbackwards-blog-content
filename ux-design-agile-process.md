# POST 7: Design Doesn't Fit in a Sprint — And That's the Real Agile Problem

**URL slug:** `/blog/ux-design-agile-process`
**Primary keyword:** UX in agile development
**Secondary keywords:** dual track agile UX, design sprints vs scrum sprints, how to include UX in agile, agile UX design process
**Meta description:** Agile was designed for engineering velocity. It was never designed for UX research. Here's what actually works — and how leading teams structure design in agile.

---

Agile works remarkably well for engineering teams managing incremental software delivery. It was never designed for UX research — and the gap between those two realities is quietly breaking design teams at organizations around the world.

The problem is simple but structurally persistent: good UX research takes time that sprint cadences do not accommodate. User interviews need to be scheduled. Research needs to be analyzed. Synthesis takes days, not hours. And the insights you need before engineering starts building cannot be produced while engineering is already building.

When organizations force design into sprint velocity, they get one of two outcomes: design that is rushed and superficial, or design that is a constant bottleneck. Neither is what leadership thought they were signing up for when they adopted Agile.

---

## How Agile Gets Applied to Design (And Why It Usually Fails)

The Scrum model assumes that work can be broken into discrete, deliverable chunks that fit inside a two-week sprint. For most engineering tasks, this works. For UX design — and especially for UX research — it often does not.

User research cannot be rushed to fit a sprint without losing the depth that makes it valuable. A discovery phase compressed into four days of interviews plus one day of synthesis produces shallow insights that engineering then builds confidently toward the wrong conclusion.

The resulting features ship. Users do not love them. The team runs retrospectives that do not identify the root cause — which is that discovery was treated like a sprint task when it needed to be its own phase.

Meanwhile, design work that should happen before engineering begins gets squeezed into the same sprint as engineering, which means designers are producing specs for engineering while simultaneously trying to validate the concepts behind those specs. This is not agile. It is chaos with better vocabulary.

---

## What Actually Works: Dual-Track Agile

Dual-track agile is the model that resolves this tension. The idea is straightforward: design (discovery track) runs ahead of engineering (delivery track), maintaining a buffer that ensures engineering always has validated, research-backed work to build toward.

In the discovery track, the design team is conducting research, generating concepts, prototyping, and testing. The output is a backlog of validated, well-specified work that engineering can pick up with confidence.

In the delivery track, engineering is building. They have the designs they need. They are not waiting on specs, and designers are not blocking them because they are working one sprint ahead.

This is not a new idea. Dual-track agile has been in practice since at least 2012. But it is still far from universal, and many organizations resist it because it requires accepting that design and engineering cannot be synchronized in the same sprint — which means accepting that design has a different rhythm than engineering.

That acceptance is worth making.

---

## The Case for Design Sprints

For organizations that need to move fast on a specific, bounded problem, the design sprint model — developed by Google Ventures and adapted widely — offers a time-boxed approach to research, ideation, and validation.

In five days, a design sprint can take a well-defined problem from zero to a tested prototype. The structure ensures that research happens before design decisions are made, that the team converges on a single direction rather than pursuing multiple paths, and that the prototype is tested with real users before any engineering commitment is made.

Our sprint work with [Tuft & Needle](https://www.drawbackwards.com/work/tuftandneedle) is a direct example. In two weeks — a compressed sprint model — the team ran research, identified the friction causing high contact volume, designed a solution, and began testing. The result was a 50% reduction in contacts per order. That speed was possible because the sprint structure forced the right questions in the right order. Research came first. Design followed. Engineering had something validated to build.

---

## What Agile Teams Get Wrong About Design

The most common mistake is treating design as a production step rather than an investigative one. In a feature factory model, design is what happens after the feature is decided and before engineering starts. The designer's job is to make the decided feature look good.

In a mature UX model, design includes the process of deciding what to build at all. The designer is not executing a brief — they are helping generate the brief through research and prototype iteration. That process cannot happen in the same sprint as engineering execution, because it needs to happen before engineering execution.

---

## Starting Points

Adopting dual-track agile does not require a process overhaul. It requires one structural shift: design needs a time buffer ahead of engineering.

Start by dedicating the first sprint of a new product area or major feature to pure discovery — no engineering, just research, mapping, and prototype generation. Let engineering begin building in sprint two, while design moves to discovery on the next area.

Over time, this rhythm becomes natural. Engineers stop asking where their specs are. Designers stop feeling behind. And users get products built around real understanding of their needs.
